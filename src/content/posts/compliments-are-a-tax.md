I'm a bit over two years into my career, working as a Business Processes Consultant at SAP. For most of time my work was on database performance consulting, but in the last four months my team started doing AI-focused services for customers — workshops, POCs, Agents development. I'm new at this part.

Around the same time, I read _The Mom Test_, Rob Fitzpatrick's book on why people are bad at giving founders honest feedback, and why compliments in particular are close to worthless as signal. It's written for founders talking to early-stage customers. I used to think 'customers' meant the same thing in every context. But they don't. The people in my rooms aren't casual users or individuals giving friendly feedback. They are enterprise buyers with their own procurement teams and decades of professional experiences. I didn't think a book written for coffee-shop startup interviews would apply to high-stakes corporate boardrooms. But I was wrong. The settings are completely different, but human nature remains exactly the same.

A recent workshop we ran played out in a way that echoed the book’s core warning almost perfectly, and it forced me to completely rethink how we talk to clients.

## The core idea

_The Mom Test_'s central claim is simple: people are bad at predicting their own behavior, and worse at being honest about your idea when you're standing in front of them. Ask "would you use this?" and you'll get "sounds great!" almost every time. The fix isn't to ask better opinion questions. It's to stop asking for opinions at all, and instead listen for specifics: what people already do, what they've already paid for, what they'd change about something that already exists in front of them.

Compliments, in other words, aren't signal. They're the tax people pay to end an uncomfortable conversation without hurting your feelings.

## The workshop

Our team spent a week preparing an AI workshop for an enterprise customer — a few hundred slides, plus several live demos and POCs. The intent, as we discussed it internally, was for the customer to genuinely understand SAP's AI and cloud products: what they were, how they worked, why the architecture made sense. Underneath that, obviously, was the hope that they'd want to purchase our product and services.

During the actual session, the pattern was hard to miss, even for someone as new to this as I am. On the slides — the concepts, the architecture, the "how it works" — the room gave us warmth. Comments like "this is very advanced," "really impressive." On the demos — actual screens and demo data — the room gave us something different: specific, opinionated feedback. How they'd want a certain product surfaced more prominently. How search results should be organized for their catalog. What a customer-facing page should show first.

One moment made the gap obvious. We were walking through vector search, and got to cosine similarity. Someone in the room, half-joking, said: "I forgot what cosine even is."

That line stuck with me more than anything on the slides did.

Afterward, thinking it over, I realized what had probably happened. The praise wasn't engagement — it was the room being professional. Nobody wants to sit through someone else's week of preparation and say "I didn't understand most of that" out loud. So they said "very advanced" instead, and the conversation moved on. The demo feedback was different, because it touched something they actually had a stake in.

We had been treating the compliments as a sign the workshop went well. Looking back, I think they meant closer to the opposite — a room that hadn't really followed the material, giving the simplest available response instead. That actually aligned with the book, just moved from a startup pitch into a room full of senior business consultants who've been doing their jobs for decades.

## Where it echoed for me

_The Mom Test_ is written around a founder talking to an individual person. My situation is different in a few ways, but the same idea stays.

**The politeness in my room had a different source.** An individual user being nice to a founder is being nice to a person they just met. The people in my workshop were maintaining a vendor relationship. That kind of politeness is harder to notice, because it doesn't look like avoidance. It looks like normal professional conduct.

**They weren't there for the AI.** Our customers were senior business consultants, not engineers, and understandably had no interest in becoming one. What they actually cared about was concrete: if they used our product, how would it change what their own customers see? Which of their products would get surfaced, and how? The demo touched that question directly. Cosine similarity did not.

**The technical explanation we were proud of was mostly noise to them.** We'd put real effort into explaining the math and the architecture, thinking it would build confidence in the product. But to customer, it has landed as a wall they had to wait out before we got back to something they could actually react to.

## What I want to try next time

I've only run a handful of these so far, so I'm cautious about calling any of this a settled practice. But a few things I want to do differently:

- **Qualify the persona before opening PowerPoint.** Before building the agenda, work with sales to clarify who is in the room. Are they technical architects curious about backend infrastructure, or business leaders focused on business scenarios? If it's the latter, completely invert the playbook: pitch the visual business outcomes first, and leave the technical architecture as a "just-in-time" appendix.
- **Notice when a room is complimenting instead of arguing.** If people are praising how advanced something is rather than pushing back on the specifics, that's probably a sign it didn't land.
- **Pay more attention to requested changes than to reactions.** When someone asks to move a product higher on a results page, that's more useful than any amount of "this is impressive," because it's specific enough to actually be wrong, and specific enough to act on.

## What I'm taking from this

The book's title is a joke that holds up: even your mother will tell you your idea is great, because she loves you. It turns out senior enterprise buyers will do something similar, for different reasons — professionalism instead of love, but a similarly unhelpful signal underneath it.

I'm still early in figuring out how to run these workshops well. But I've stopped treating a room full of nodding as a good sign, and started paying closer attention to the moments someone asks me to change something specific on screen.
